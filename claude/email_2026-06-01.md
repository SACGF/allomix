VCF CALLING ISSUES

Wendy pointed out that the panel I used had been trimmed to be close to exons, missing some intron markers - switched to using the full panel, gaining us more markers.

I had to modify the VCF calling slightly - the basics are:

* Joint call host/donor germline
* Look at allele depth at those sites in the admixture sample

But there were complexities:

* GATK sometimes holds back on a germline call thinking it's an error, but if you pass enough samples in, it can see that it's real - have passed in gnomAD >5% AF variants. This has rescued some borderline HET calls and gains us a few more markers.
* GATK has filters on very rare AD calls (as do almost all variant callers, thinking them errors) which wrecks our very low-LOD. Solution is to use "pileup" which just gives raw allele depth counts rather than any sophisticated variant calling / filtering

TP53 UP LITERATURE SEARCH

I figured that surely if this happens, someone has noticed this is a defining feature of relapse?

Did a "deep research" workflow - 102 agents on web search - 1.5M tokens, came back with:

  1. The confounder is old and well-established

  LOH/CNV at marker loci distorting chimerism is textbook, mostly in STR assays. Loci on gained/lost chromosomes are routinely excluded because
  copy-number change at a marker skews the percentage, and clonal LOH erasing heterozygosity distorts the recipient fraction.
  - Vietz/Lin et al., PMC3219907: "Chromosomal gain or loss obviously affects STR analysis and may lead to errors."
  - ASH 2018, Blood 132 Suppl 1:5135: an ALL patient with LOH in 10/15 het STR loci distorting recipient chimerism.
  - UK NEQAS, Br J Haematol 2014 (10.1111/bjh.13073): lists LOH in relapse samples as a source of chimerism variability.

  So the mechanism — LOH moves the per-marker signal — is independently confirmed, exactly as I argued from your donor pileup.
  
  2. The feature version exists, but only narrowly
  
  Two forms, neither of which is what you're doing:
  - STR, inverted into a relapse marker — Vietz/Lin (PMC3219907) explicitly say "STR loci showing altered copy number... can provide evidence of residual
  cancer cells... a marker of impending relapse." But it's a single n=4 case series, STR-specific, and the authors call it less sensitive than RT-PCR.
  - NGS HLA-loss assays — this is the real precedent. Devyser HLA Loss (Front Immunol 2025, PMC12259423) and HLA-CLN (Cell Transplant 2022,
  10.1177/09636897221102902) report per-marker chimerism and flag the HLA region deviating below the rest of the genome to detect CN-LOH/acquired UPD of 
  6p as immune-escape relapse (concept from Vago, Blood 2009;115:3158). This is your idea's mechanism — per-marker allelic imbalance for clonal LOH
  within a chimerism assay — but locked to the HLA/6p region by design, not incidental genome-wide detection at arbitrary driver loci.

  3. Your exact thing — TP53/17p at an incidental SNP — appears to be a genuine gap
  
  - The TP53/17p biology is rock-solid (Jasek, Leukemia 2009, PMID 19759556: "TP53 mutations... are either homozygous or hemizygous due to CN-LOH or
  deletion of 17p"; Sugimoto, Leuk Res 2016, PMID 26851439: CN-LOH 17p in 18% of MDS) — but those are diagnostic/MDS-biology papers, not chimerism
  papers. They prove the mechanism, not the application.
  - Mixed chimerism + MRD integration is established, but always genome-wide-average chimerism paired with a separate MRD readout (Kanaan, Blood Adv
  2023; FIGARO/Pearce, Blood Adv 2023; Lee/Jo, J Clin Med 2019 — a combined NGS chimerism+MRD panel that averages 121 SNPs). None use per-marker LOH.
  - And tellingly, mainstream NGS-SNP chimerism treats chromosomal deletion as a false-negative to dilute out with more markers — AlloSeq HCT review
  (PMC10380370): "the high number of markers avoids false-negative results caused by chromosomal deletions." The AlloSeq-vs-STR validation (Kim/Stahl, J
  Mol Diagn 2024, PMID 38307253) never mentions CNV, LOH, TP53, or allelic imbalance at all. The field's posture is the exact opposite of exploiting the 
  per-locus signal.

  Two nuances that matter for us
  
  1. Direction. The literature is almost entirely about deletion → reduced recipient signal (false-negative). You're seeing the over-representation
  direction (CN-LOH duplicating the retained allele → signal up), which is the expected UPD signature but is even less characterized in the chimerism
  literature. So the specific thing you observed sits in a blind spot of a blind spot.
  2. The HLA-loss assays are your roadmap, not your competition. They prove that "per-marker imbalance as a feature" is real, validated, and clinically
  used in NGS — just regionally hardcoded. allomix already exposes per-marker chimerism genome-wide (that's literally what the Manhattan/per-marker
  tooling does), so generalizing the HLA-loss idea to any driver locus is the novel, defensible step.
 
  Honest caveats

  This is a web search, so "appears novel" ≠ proven novel — a proper PubMed/Scholar sweep and a patent check are warranted before you'd claim it. Two
  sub-claims got refuted in verification and should not be cited (a "12% vs 2%" HLA-loss discordance figure, and a stronger "HLA-CLN is a general-LOH
  precedent" framing). And it's still a hypothesis until the timepoint test — does TP53 host signal rise across serial samples in relapsers? — which the
  literature says is exactly the discriminator you'd need.
  
  The cleanest takeaway for the build: the field already knows you must separate true clonal imbalance from per-marker technical bias (the STR people use
  per-locus baselines; the HLA assays compare region-vs-rest). That's precisely the calibrated per-marker null / overdispersion modeling we kept
  circling (Step 14 error table, Step 24 rho) — so the same machinery that cleans up your quantification is what would make the relapse-feature rigorous.
