## Results

### In Silico Validation Across Sequencing Depths

To assess allomix accuracy under realistic conditions, we generated synthetic chimeric VCFs across donor fractions from 0% to 100% using 100 biallelic SNP markers (80 informative) with per-marker capture bias drawn from the empirically calibrated heavy-tailed mixture model (see Methods), non-uniform depth (CV = 0.43), and 1.6% locus dropout. We repeated the validation at five sequencing depths (50x, 100x, 200x, 500x, and 1,000x) spanning the range encountered in clinical panels, from low-depth whole-exome capture to high-depth targeted amplicon sequencing. At each depth, {{ depth_50.n_replicates | dp(0) }} independent replicates were generated with different random seeds for per-marker biases and sampling noise.

allomix accurately estimated donor fraction across all depths and mixture levels (Figure 1). Estimation precision improved with increasing sequencing depth. At 50x depth, the mean absolute error (MAE) was {{ depth_50.mean_abs_error_pct | dp(2) }} ± {{ depth_50.mean_abs_error_sd_pct | dp(2) }}% (mean ± SD across replicates) with RMSE {{ depth_50.rmse_pct | dp(2) }} ± {{ depth_50.rmse_sd_pct | dp(2) }}%, while at 1,000x depth these improved to {{ depth_1000.mean_abs_error_pct | dp(2) }} ± {{ depth_1000.mean_abs_error_sd_pct | dp(2) }}% MAE and {{ depth_1000.rmse_pct | dp(2) }} ± {{ depth_1000.rmse_sd_pct | dp(2) }}% RMSE (Table 1, Figure 2). MAE remained below 1% at all depths tested (Figure 3).

### Effect of Per-Marker Bias Correction

To evaluate the impact of per-marker amplification bias correction, we generated synthetic chimeric samples at 2,000x depth with known per-marker biases ($\sigma_{bias}$ = 0.02) and ran allomix both with and without bias correction using the true bias values.

Without bias correction, the mean absolute error on interior fractions was {{ bias_no_bias.mean_abs_error_pct | dp(2) }}% with RMSE {{ bias_no_bias.rmse_pct | dp(2) }}%. With bias correction the mean absolute error was {{ bias_with_bias.mean_abs_error_pct | dp(2) }}% with RMSE {{ bias_with_bias.rmse_pct | dp(2) }}%, a small improvement, and the mean confidence interval narrowed from {{ bias_no_bias.mean_ci_width_pct | dp(2) }}% to {{ bias_with_bias.mean_ci_width_pct | dp(2) }}%. The boundary fraction estimates were essentially unchanged: the 0% and 100% donor samples were estimated at {{ bias_with_bias.est_0pct }}% and {{ bias_with_bias.est_100pct }}% both with and without correction. The benefit is modest by design: the correction is applied in logit space (Methods), so at the extreme expected allele frequencies that dominate boundary and low-donor fractions it is a small proportional adjustment rather than a large shift, and most of its effect falls on interior fractions near VAF 0.5.

### Confidence Interval Calibration

The 95% profile likelihood confidence intervals from the beta-binomial model showed coverage of {{ depth_1000.ci_coverage_pct }}--{{ depth_200.ci_coverage_pct }}% across depths (Table 1, Figure 3), below the nominal 95% level. The beta-binomial likelihood accounts for overdispersion via a shared concentration parameter rho. Per-marker bias correction narrows the intervals without changing coverage in the 2,000x experiment (mean width {{ bias_no_bias.mean_ci_width_pct | dp(2) }}% uncorrected versus {{ bias_with_bias.mean_ci_width_pct | dp(2) }}% corrected, both at {{ bias_with_bias.ci_coverage_pct }}% coverage). The remaining gap to nominal coverage at lower depths reflects noise sources that a single shared rho cannot fully capture: locus dropout, heavy-tailed per-marker bias distributions where overdispersion varies across loci, and depth non-uniformity. Empirical CI recalibration from training data is a potential route to closing this gap.

### Limit of Detection

To situate allomix against the limit-of-detection specifications cited by commercial vendors, we characterised LoD across panel size, sequencing depth, and donor-host relatedness using the EP17-A2 framework (see Methods). We separated the two sources of variation: for each relatedness level we drew multiple donor/host pairs ({{ lod_headline.n_pairs_unrelated }} unrelated, {{ lod_headline.n_pairs_sibling }} sibling), and for each pair we generated {{ lod_headline.n_seq_reps }} sequencing replicates per cell. The limit of blank (LoB, 95th percentile of estimated donor fraction on a pure-host sample) and limit of detection (LoD, lowest true fraction at which $\geq$95% of replicates exceed LoB) were estimated per pair from a logistic fit over the dilution series, and we report the median LoD across pairs with a 10th-90th percentile across-pair band (Figure 7).

At an unrelated donor, 100 markers, and 1,000x mean depth, the in silico LoD was {{ lod_headline.unrelated_lod_1000x_100markers_pct }}% donor fraction; with full-sibling donors at the same panel and depth, the LoD rose to {{ lod_headline.sibling_lod_1000x_100markers_pct }}%, reflecting the reduction in informative markers (Table 3). Doubling the panel size to 200 markers at 500x depth yielded an unrelated LoD of {{ lod_headline.unrelated_lod_500x_200markers_pct }}%; pushing to 400 markers at 2,000x reduced unrelated LoD to {{ lod_headline.unrelated_lod_2000x_400markers_pct }}% (sibling {{ lod_headline.sibling_lod_2000x_400markers_pct }}%). The LoD curves decrease monotonically in both panel size and depth (Figure 7), allowing laboratories with a panel of *X* markers at *Y* mean depth to read off the expected in silico LoD for their assay.

### Recipient Copy-Number Aberrations

The HSCT recipient is usually a haematological malignancy patient, and the residual or relapsing recipient clone commonly carries somatic copy-number changes: copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity (CN-LoH, where the clone retains two copies of one parental homolog), deletions, and gains. The diploid mixture model does not represent these, so an affected marker carries a biased allele fraction. We simulated recipient CN-LoH, deletion (one retained copy), and gain (three copies) at a controllable burden (fraction of eligible markers affected) and measured the effect on the limit of detection in the two clinically relevant directions, using the same EP17-A2 framework as above ({{ cnv_loh_headline.ref_markers }} markers, {{ cnv_loh_headline.ref_depth }}x depth, {{ cnv_loh_headline.n_reps_per_frac }} replicates per cell; Methods, Figure 8).

For relapse early-warning, where the recipient clone is the minor component being detected, recipient copy-number aberrations had little effect. The relapse limit of detection was {{ cnv_loh_headline.relapse_lod_baseline_unrel_pct | dp(2) }}% (unrelated) and {{ cnv_loh_headline.relapse_lod_baseline_sib_pct | dp(2) }}% (sibling) with no aberration, and stayed at or below {{ cnv_loh_headline.relapse_lod_max_pct | dp(2) }}% across every aberration type and burden (Figure 8, top row). This insensitivity is expected from the design: the limit of blank is set on a pure-donor sample with no aberration present, and the markers that carry the recipient signal most strongly are recipient-homozygous, which CN-LoH (a heterozygous-site effect) does not alter.

The opposite direction, detecting a donor against a substantial aberration-bearing recipient background (mixed chimerism, or quantifying a small donor fraction), was strongly degraded. From a baseline donor LoD of {{ cnv_loh_headline.donor_lod_baseline_unrel_pct | dp(2) }}% (unrelated) and {{ cnv_loh_headline.donor_lod_baseline_sib_pct | dp(2) }}% (sibling), even a 10% CN-LoH burden pushed the unrelated donor LoD above the 20% probed ceiling without the outlier-resistant refit, and deletion did the same (donor no longer detectable; Figure 8, bottom row). Deletion was the most damaging, because it skews allele balance, halves the locus DNA contribution, and affects every genotype rather than only heterozygous sites; gain was the mildest, inflating the donor LoD only to a few percent. The median/MAD outlier-resistant refit applied by default (Methods) recovered much of this loss at low to moderate burden: it returned the high-burden gain donor LoD from {{ cnv_loh_headline.donor_lod_gain_high_unrel_std_pct | dp(2) }}% to {{ cnv_loh_headline.donor_lod_gain_high_unrel_robust_pct | dp(2) }}% and lowered the low-burden deletion and CN-LoH LoDs toward baseline (Figure 8, bottom row, dashed lines), while leaving aberration-free samples unchanged. It cannot recover CN-LoH or deletion once the burden is high enough that the aberrant markers are no longer a minority; the estimator flags these samples for review (Methods).

### Effect of Donor-Host Relatedness

In clinical HSCT, donors may be unrelated, or may be relatives of the host, with siblings being the most common related donor type. Increased relatedness reduces the number of informative markers (where donor and host genotypes differ), potentially degrading chimerism estimation. To evaluate this, we generated synthetic donor-host pairs at four relatedness levels (unrelated, first cousin, half-sibling, full sibling) using population allele frequencies (MAF 0.2–0.5), with {{ rel_unrelated.n_replicates }} replicate pairs per level and {{ rel_unrelated.n_markers }} markers at 500x depth (Figure 4, Table 3).

The mean number of informative markers decreased with increasing relatedness, from {{ rel_unrelated.mean_informative }} (unrelated) to {{ rel_sibling.mean_informative }} (full sibling). Despite this reduction, allomix maintained sub-2% mean absolute error across all relatedness levels: {{ rel_unrelated.mean_mae_pct }}% (unrelated), {{ rel_cousin.mean_mae_pct }}% (cousin), {{ rel_half_sibling.mean_mae_pct }}% (half-sibling), and {{ rel_sibling.mean_mae_pct }}% (sibling). Even in the worst case (sibling donors), the minimum number of informative markers observed was {{ rel_sibling.min_informative }}, well above the minimum of 3 required for estimation.

### Multi-Donor Estimation with Sibling Donors

Of {{ multidonor.n_markers | dp(0) }} total markers, {{ multidonor.n_informative_any | dp(0) }} were informative for at least one donor ({{ multidonor.n_informative_d1 | dp(0) }} for donor 1, {{ multidonor.n_informative_d2 | dp(0) }} for donor 2), reflecting the expected reduction due to full-sibling relatedness.

allomix was run on {{ multidonor.n_samples | dp(0) }} chimeric samples spanning the simplex of (donor 1, donor 2) fractions, including pure host, pure single-donor, balanced mixes, and asymmetric mixes (Table 4, Figure 5). Per-donor mean absolute error was {{ multidonor.mae_d1_pct }}% for donor 1 (RMSE {{ multidonor.rmse_d1_pct }}%) and {{ multidonor.mae_d2_pct }}% for donor 2 (RMSE {{ multidonor.rmse_d2_pct }}%), both well below 2%. ### Longitudinal Timeline Monitoring

To demonstrate allomix's support for serial chimerism monitoring, the primary clinical use case, we simulated a post-HSCT engraftment trajectory across {{ timeline.n_timepoints | dp(0) }} timepoints from day +14 to day +365 (Figure 6). The scenario includes early engraftment (15% donor at day +14), progressive engraftment through day +100 (95% donor), a clinically relevant dip at day +180 (92% donor, representing possible mixed chimerism that would trigger clinical attention), and recovery to 97% donor at day +365. Synthetic chimeric VCFs were generated at {{ timeline.depth | commas }}x depth with empirically calibrated noise and {{ timeline.n_replicates | dp(0) }} independent replicates. allomix tracked the trajectory accurately, with mean absolute error {{ timeline.mae_pct | dp(2) }} ± {{ timeline.mae_sd_pct | dp(2) }}% across all timepoints (maximum {{ timeline.max_error_pct | dp(2) }} ± {{ timeline.max_error_sd_pct | dp(2) }}%). The day +180 dip was correctly detected in all replicates, with a mean absolute error of {{ timeline.dip_abs_error_pct | dp(2) }}% at that timepoint.
